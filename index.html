What is the Judicial System?

The Judicial Branch is one of three branches of the US government.
The job of the Judicial Branch is to interpret laws.

What is a court?

Basics of Court

A court is a place where a judge or jury hears a legal case. Basically, it's a building where a judge decides on a conflict. Judges interpret the law, control hearings and trials, and practice being impartial for the better good of the country. A jury is a group of regular, common citizens who decide on the case. A trial is the first hearing of a court case.

What kind of cases are there?

Type 1: Criminal

A criminal case is when a person breaks the law. They can be charged for committing a crime and possibly be sentenced to be put in jail. There are 2 types of criminal cases: a felony and a misdemeanor. A felony is a major crime, and the criminal can be sentenced to over a year in jail. A misdemeanor is a minor crime, and the criminal can only be sentenced to one year in jail or less. To help determine if a crime was actually committed or not, witnesses, or people who saw or heard you do something related to the crime, might either testify for or against you.

What is the other kind of case?

Type 2: Civil

A civil case is when two people are having a dispute. The judge is trying to resolve the conflict, or to end the dispute between the two parties. The plaintiff is trying to sue someone, or to bring up a civil case against someone. The person being sued is called the defendant. (In both criminal and civil cases, the defendant is the person who is charged of a crime.)

What happens after the judge/jury hears the case?

Type 2: Civil

The jury has to reach a verdict, or a decision whether you are guilty or not, which has to be decided by every member of the jury. If the defendant is lucky, they will have an acquittal, which is when you are deemed not guilty and are free to go. However, if the defendant is deemed guilty, they have the option to appeal. To appeal is to reject the court's decision and have a higher court hear the case. This means that the defendant is not satisified with the final decision.

Are there famous cases that I should know about?

Landmark Cases

Certain cases that go up to the Supreme Court, or the highest court in the United States, become landmark cases. Landmark cases are cases of legal importance, settling a significant matter, and the decision is followed by the judiciary to decide future cases dealing with the same issue. This means that the Supreme Court's decision helps determine the decisions for similar cases in the future.


What was one of the landmark cases?

Miranda v. Arizona

Ernesto Miranda was arrested in Phoenix, Arizona. Officers interrogated him for two hours. Miranda ended up signing a written confession. In court, Miranda argued that his confession was not voluntary because the officers did not tell him he could remain silent. In the end, the Supreme Court agreed that Miranda's 5th Amendment right was violated. He was not aware of his rights first. So, this led to the famous Miranda warning that cops tell criminals on TV, and also in real life.

What is another one?

Gideon v. Wainwright

Clarence Earl Gideon was charged with breaking and entering and theft. In court, he had to defend himself without a lawyer, since he could not afford one. Gideon sent a petition to the Supreme Court saying his 6th Amendment right, that all criminals should have the right to a lawyer, was violated. The Supreme Court agreed, and there was a re-trial. With a lawyer, Gideon was proved not guilty. Now, public defenders, or lawyers who work for the state to help criminal defendants, are given to defendants who cannot afford their own lawyer.

State Courts

The Family Court is a specialized court for hearing domestic issues, such as family issues or custody of children. District Court hears less serious cases, while Circuit Court hears more serious cases. If a defendant appeals, then the case goes to the Intermediate Court of Appeals. If the defendant appeals again, the case goes to the State Supreme Court.

Federal Courts

The US District Court hears all types of cases. If the defendant appeals, the case goes to the US Circuit Court of Appeals. If the defendant appeals again, the case goes to the US Supreme Court.
